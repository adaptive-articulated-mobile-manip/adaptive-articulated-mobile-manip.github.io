Adaptive Mobile Manipulation for Articulated Objects
In the Open World

Abstract

Deploying robots in open-ended unstructured environments such as homes has been a long-standing research problem. However, robots are often studied only in closed-off lab settings, and prior mobile manipulation work is restricted to pick-move-place, which is arguably just the tip of the iceberg in this area. In this paper, we introduce Open-World Mobile Manipulation System, a \textbf{full-stack} approach to tackle realistic articulated object operation, e.g. real-world doors, cabinets, drawers, and refrigerators in open-ended unstructured environments. We introduce an adaptive learning framework to initially learn from a small set of data through behavior cloning (BC), followed by learning from online adaptation on novel objects that fall outside the BC training distribution. We also develop a low-cost mobile manipulation hardware platform capable of safe and autonomous adaptation in unstructured environments with a cost of around $25,000$ USD. We validate the effectiveness of our system by conducting experiments on 20 articulate objects across 4 buildings on the university campus.



Online Adaptation.

We start by initializing our policy using a limited set of training data through behavior cloning. The central challenge we face is operating with new objects that fall outside the training domain. To address this, we've developed a system capable of fully autonomous Reinforcement Learning (RL) adaptation, utilizing a Vision-Language Model (VLM) for reward function In a trial period of less than one hour, this system demonstrated significant improvement, boosting the success rate from 50% to 95% without any human intervention.
